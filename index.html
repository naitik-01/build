My journey

For most of my school life, I was an average student. There was no “child prodigy” tag, no guaranteed IIT/AIIMS storyline. Things changed after 10th, when I had to choose my stream: PCB or PCM. I chose PCM first, almost by instinct, and then the real turning point arrived.

In the first FA‑1 of 11th, I scored 99.8% in PCM. That one result completely changed my confidence. Instead of playing safe, I added PCB as well and became a rare PCBM student in my school – out of the entire batch, only two of us had PCBM, and I was one of them. From 11th and 12th, the “average student” became the topper of both PCB and PCBM streams.

After 12th, I stood at a familiar Indian crossroads: medical vs engineering. I prepared for NEET, but also decided to appear for both JEE Main and NEET. In my first attempt, I cleared JEE Main, qualified for JEE Advanced, and also qualified NEET. However, my NEET score wasn’t enough for a government MBBS seat, while JEE was giving me government engineering options in distant cities like Gorakhpur and Kanpur. Instead of going far from home for a branch I wasn’t fully excited about, I chose a different path: a top private engineering college close to my ecosystem.

That decision brought me to MIET Meerut, into the core CSE branch. From an engineering background, there was no one in my family; I was the first one to enter this line. So the journey from there was all about figuring things out from scratch.

In my first year itself, I took a risk and participated in my first ever ideathon – AICTE Ideathon 2023. I didn’t go with big expectations, but I ended up in the top 3 and received a cash prize. That small win validated something important: I wasn’t just a student anymore; I could think, pitch and build.

Opportunities started compounding. I was selected as an IIT Bombay E‑Cell Campus Ambassador, and later entered Starthon 2k24, a startup pitching competition, where once again I was among the top 2. From there, I kept participating in national and international competitions, each time refining my ideas, decks and confidence.

Parallel to this, I started building my own ecosystem. I founded Tech4Hack, which has now grown into a national‑level community with 3000+ members and a reach of over 50,000 people across platforms.[web:6][web:7][web:22] On LinkedIn, I crossed 5,000+ followers, using the platform to share my learnings, events and opportunities for other students as well.

The roles around me also evolved. I went from being a hackathon participant to a hackathon mentor (2×) and organizer, and from attending Microsoft events to being a speaker at Microsoft (2×). Similarly, I first attended DevFest as an audience member; the next year, I came back on the organizing side. In college, I started as a volunteer and team member in 1st and 2nd year; by 3rd year, three+ clubs were working under me, and I was leading them, including the MS Azure Centre of Excellence and being an IEEE member.

Somewhere along this path, “doing college” quietly transformed into building a startup and communities. Along with my community work and leadership roles, I also started my own startup, tying together everything I had learned from ideathons, starthons, hackathons and campus initiatives into something real and scalable.

From an “average” 10th‑class student to a PCBM topper, from confused between NEET and JEE to a CSE student leader, from a first‑time ideathon participant to a founder, speaker and mentor – this is the journey that defines who I am and what Naitik builds.
